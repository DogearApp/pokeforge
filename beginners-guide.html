Pokemon Champions Beginner's Guide to Competitive VGC Doubles

Updated: April 4, 2026By InciniForge10 min read

Pokemon Champions is the first Pokemon game designed specifically for competitive battles. If you're new to VGC (Video Game Championships) doubles, this guide will teach you everything you need to go from casual player to competitive battler.

What is VGC Doubles?

VGC (Video Game Championships) is the official competitive Pokemon format run by The Pokemon Company. Here's how it works:

  • Doubles format — Each player sends out 2 Pokemon at a time (not 1 like in-game battles)
  • Bring 6, choose 4 — You build a team of 6 Pokemon, but only bring 4 to each battle. You choose which 4 after seeing your opponent's team.
  • Level 50 — All Pokemon are set to Level 50 for competitive battles
  • Item clause — No two Pokemon on your team can hold the same item
  • Species clause — No duplicate Pokemon on the same team
  • One Mega Evolution per team — You can only Mega Evolve one Pokemon per battle
Key Difference from Singles: In doubles, you need to think about BOTH your Pokemon every turn. Spread moves (like Earthquake and Heat Wave) hit multiple targets. Positioning and prediction matter twice as much.

Understanding EVs (Effort Values)

EVs are hidden stat boosts that make the difference between a casual Pokemon and a competitive one.

  • Every Pokemon can have a total of 508 EVs spread across 6 stats
  • Each stat can have a maximum of 252 EVs
  • Every 4 EVs in a stat adds 1 point to that stat at Level 50
  • A common spread is 252/252/4 — max two stats, put the last 4 somewhere else

Example: A Garchomp with 252 Atk / 252 Spe / 4 HP hits as hard as possible and is as fast as possible. A Garchomp with no EVs has ~30 less Attack and ~30 less Speed — that's the difference between a KO and not.

Beginner Tip: Start with simple 252/252/4 spreads. As you improve, you'll learn to customize EV spreads to survive specific attacks (called "benchmarking").

Understanding Natures

Natures boost one stat by 10% and reduce another by 10%. Always pick a nature that boosts your Pokemon's most important stat and reduces one it doesn't use.

  • Physical attackers: Adamant (+Atk / -SpA) or Jolly (+Spe / -SpA)
  • Special attackers: Modest (+SpA / -Atk) or Timid (+Spe / -Atk)
  • Bulky supports: Impish (+Def / -SpA), Careful (+SpD / -SpA), Bold (+Def / -Atk), or Calm (+SpD / -Atk)
Rule of Thumb: If your Pokemon uses physical moves, lower SpA. If it uses special moves, lower Atk. If it's fast, use a Speed-boosting nature (Jolly/Timid).

The 5 Essential Moves in Doubles

1. Protect

The most important move in VGC. Protect blocks all attacks for one turn. In doubles, this lets your partner attack freely while the opponent has to guess which Pokemon to target. Put Protect on almost every Pokemon.

2. Fake Out

Priority flinch move that only works on the first turn out. Guarantees your partner gets a free turn to set up Tailwind, Trick Room, or attack. Incineroar is the most common user.

3. Tailwind

Doubles your entire side's Speed for 4 turns. Winning the speed battle is crucial in doubles. Common setters: Whimsicott (Prankster priority), Pelipper, Tornadus.

4. Trick Room

Reverses turn order for 5 turns — slower Pokemon move first. This is an entire team archetype. Common setters: Hatterene, Farigiraf, Porygon2, Dusclops.

5. Earthquake/Heat Wave/Rock Slide

Spread moves that hit both opponents. In doubles, spread moves deal 75% damage to each target but hitting 2 Pokemon means 150% total value. Be careful — Earthquake also hits your partner.

Team Building Basics

A good VGC team needs these 6 roles covered:

  1. Speed control — Tailwind setter or Trick Room setter (Whimsicott, Hatterene)
  2. Physical attacker — Strong Attack stat with good moves (Garchomp, Kingambit)
  3. Special attacker — Strong Special Attack to hit physically bulky Pokemon (Dragapult, Hydreigon)
  4. Defensive support — Intimidate, screens, redirection (Incineroar, Grimmsnarl)
  5. Mega Evolution — Your team's power spike (Mega Metagross, Mega Charizard Y)
  6. Flexible 6th — Covers weaknesses in your team's type coverage
The Golden Rule: Check your type coverage. Use a tool like InciniForge to make sure you can hit every type super effectively and don't share too many weaknesses.

Mega Evolutions in Champions

Mega Evolutions are back in Pokemon Champions. Key rules:

  • Only one Pokemon per team can Mega Evolve per battle
  • Mega Evolution happens at the start of the turn, before moves execute
  • The Pokemon must hold its specific Mega Stone (can't use any other item)
  • Stats change immediately, but abilities may change from the pre-mega ability
  • Intimidate trick: Pokemon like Gyarados and Salamence have Intimidate before Mega Evolving. Send them out, get the Intimidate, THEN Mega Evolve for a different ability.

Speed Tiers — Why They Matter

In doubles, moving first is often the difference between winning and losing. Here are the key speed benchmarks in Champions:

  • Very Fast (130+): Dragapult (142), Weavile (125), Greninja (122), Talonflame (126)
  • Fast (100-129): Garchomp (102), Lucario (90), Hydreigon (98), Arcanine (95)
  • Medium (60-99): Incineroar (60), Milotic (81), Kommo-o (85)
  • Slow (under 60): Torkoal (20), Hatterene (29), Kingambit (50)

Your First Competitive Team

Here's a simple, effective team to start with:

  1. Incineroar — Intimidate + Fake Out. The best support Pokemon. (Impish, 252 HP / 188 Def / 76 SpD, Assault Vest)
  2. Whimsicott — Prankster Tailwind. Guaranteed speed control. (Timid, 252 HP / 252 Spe, Focus Sash)
  3. Garchomp — Physical sweeper with Mega Evolution. (Jolly, 252 Atk / 252 Spe, Garchompite)
  4. Dragapult — Special attacker with incredible speed. (Timid, 252 SpA / 252 Spe, Life Orb)
  5. Kingambit — Late-game cleaner with Supreme Overlord. (Adamant, 252 HP / 252 Atk, Assault Vest)
  6. Aegislash — Versatile Steel/Ghost with stance change. (Quiet, 252 HP / 252 SpA, Weakness Policy)
How to play it: Lead Whimsicott + Incineroar. Fake Out the biggest threat, set Tailwind. Then bring in Garchomp and Dragapult to sweep under doubled speed. Kingambit cleans up late game.

Common Mistakes Beginners Make

  • Not running Protect — Protect is essential on almost every Pokemon in doubles. Use it.
  • Ignoring team preview — You get to see your opponent's 6 Pokemon before choosing your 4. Plan your leads based on what you see.
  • Not having speed control — If you don't have Tailwind or Trick Room, faster teams will destroy you every time.
  • Duplicate weaknesses — If 3 of your 6 Pokemon are weak to Ground, one Earthquake ends the game. Check your coverage.
  • Not using Fake Out — Turn 1 Fake Out is free disruption. Always have a Fake Out user.
  • Hitting your own partner — Earthquake hits your ally too! Pair it with a Flying-type or Levitate user.
